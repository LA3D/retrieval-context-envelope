Data appendix

Rank tables, gold-only comparisons, and methodology notes for the claims on the landing page.

Modified

July 11, 2026

This page is the empirical backing for the landing page. It is versioned alongside the code that produces the numbers. If a value changes on re-run, the value here changes, and the commit history records why. Values marked in progress are pending on runs that have not completed at the time of writing.

Setup

The corpus

The evaluation runs on MuSiQue (Trivedi et al., 2022), a multi-hop question-answering benchmark constructed by chaining single-hop questions from SQuAD, T-REx, Zero Shot RE, Natural Questions, and MLQA. The version used here is the answerable split (2-, 3-, and 4-hop questions with grounded supporting evidence).

  • Full corpus: on the order of 21,000 candidate paragraphs across all questions.
  • Gold-only subset: 281 questions, restricted to the paragraphs that contain the supporting evidence. This isolates the retrieval-ranking problem from the recall problem.
  • Held-out test set: 90 questions used for end-to-end answer quality.

Numbers are being validated on the full 21k corpus at the time of writing. The qualitative shape (rank rising monotonically with hop depth, most 3rd- and 4th-hop chunks outside K = 30) is stable across pilot runs. Point estimates are refreshed here as final runs complete.

The value of the table is not any single point estimate. It is the pattern: a common production cutoff of K = 10 does not include the supporting evidence for most reasoning-hop-3 and reasoning-hop-4 chunks. Even K = 30, near the lost-in-the-middle ceiling (Liu et al., 2024), does not close the gap.

Comparison

Gold-only: dense vs graph-informed

The gold-only subset lets us compare two retrieval strategies without the recall confound. Both strategies see the same paragraphs; the only difference is how they rank them.

  • Dense: cosine similarity between question embedding and paragraph embeddings, single-shot.
  • Graph-informed: RWR-based re-ranking against a graph built from the corpus. Nodes are chunks, concepts, and documents; edges come from co-occurring concepts, document layout, and (where present) cross-document links. Restart probability and edge weights are tuned on a development split.

Methodology

How the graph is built

For MuSiQue, no wiki was written by hand. The graph is extracted from the corpus:

  1. Concept extraction: entity recognition over each paragraph, keeping named entities and top-k keyphrases per paragraph.
  2. Concept-concept edges: co-occurrence within a paragraph, weighted by pointwise mutual information across the corpus.
  3. Chunk-concept edges: each paragraph is a chunk; edges connect the chunk to every extracted concept in it.
  4. Document-chunk edges: paragraphs from the same source document are linked to a shared document node.

The walk operates on the resulting tripartite graph.

For projects that already ship linked notes (a wiki with [[link]] syntax and typed frontmatter), steps 1 to 3 are unnecessary: the concept graph is the wiki graph. This is the setting where the approach is cheapest.

Caveats

What we are not claiming

  • This does not replace dense retrieval. Dense similarity is what seeds the walk. The claim is that walk-informed re-ranking closes the gap on hops that dense-alone misses.
  • This has not been evaluated on all corpora. MuSiQue is a stress test built for multi-hop; results there do not automatically generalize to single-hop domains or to corpora where evidence is not graph-structured.
  • Full-scale accuracy numbers are pending. The rank-distribution collapse on the 21k-chunk full corpus is observed. The end-to-end answer accuracy of graph-informed retrieval at full scale is being validated at time of writing.
  • GraphRAG and community-based methods are related. (Edge et al., 2024) Hierarchical community summaries are a different way to solve the same envelope-assembly problem. Which method wins depends on corpus structure; both beat plain dense at scale.
