Data appendix

The K=10 per-hop coverage table behind the landing page, and the methodology it comes from.

Modified

July 11, 2026

This page is the empirical backing for the landing page, scoped to the one comparison that carries the argument: what dense retrieval actually delivers to the language model at the production cutoff of K = 10 on a realistic multi-hop corpus. It is versioned alongside the code that produces the numbers.

Setup

The corpus

The evaluation runs on MuSiQue (Trivedi et al., 2022), a multi-hop question-answering benchmark constructed by chaining single-hop questions from SQuAD, T-REx, Zero Shot RE, Natural Questions, and MLQA. The version used here is the answerable split, 2-, 3-, and 4-hop questions with grounded supporting evidence.

  • Corpus: 21,100 candidate paragraphs, the union of every question’s supporting passages plus MuSiQue’s distractor pool.
  • Held-out test set: 90 questions, 30 each at 2-, 3-, and 4-hop.
  • Retriever: plain dense RAG, cosine similarity between question and paragraph embeddings, mpnet, L2-normalized. No entity layer, no walk, no sub-questions. This is what you get out of the box.

The result

Per-hop coverage at K = 10

g_i is the i-th-closest gold chunk for a question: g_1 is the gold chunk the retriever ranks highest, g_2 the next, and so on. The table below reports the fraction of TEST questions whose slot-i gold chunk lands inside the top 10 of the retriever’s ranking. Numbers are computed directly from cache/p3v5/vanilla_dense_21k.json.

Hop group g1 g2 g3 g4
2-hop 100 % 17 %
3-hop 97 % 37 % 0 %
4-hop 87 % 23 % 3 % 0 %

Dense retrieval reliably delivers the direct-match chunk (g_1) at every hop count, and everything else falls off a cliff. For a 4-hop question, the language model gets one gold chunk out of the four the answer chain requires. The deepest chunk lands inside K = 10 for zero of thirty questions. The gap is not that the deep chunks are missing from the corpus. They are ranked into the thousands: the deepest 4-hop chunk sits at median rank 1,796 out of 21,100.

Why not raise K?

Why K = 30 is the ceiling

Lost-in-the-middle (Liu et al., 2024) shows a U-shaped attention curve: language models attend well to the start and end of a long context and poorly to the middle. The penalty is measurable across both open-source and commercial models. Around K = 30 the middle-position attention penalty starts costing more accuracy than the extra recall buys back. Above K = 30 you are trading retriever recall against the model’s ability to use what you retrieved.

K = 10 is the production cutoff most systems settle at because it sits under the U-curve entirely, every chunk in a position the model attends to well. The now-standard pattern is retrieve a large candidate pool with a cheap dense-or-BM25 hybrid, then rerank down to K = 10 with a cross-encoder. That helps for shallow multi-hop where the missing chunk is in the initial pool. It cannot help the 4-hop deepest chunk: a chunk ranked at 1,796 is not in any candidate pool a reranker would receive.

Caveats

What we are not claiming

  • Dense retrieval is not broken. For single-hop questions and small corpora it works well. The claim is scoped: multi-hop over a realistic distractor pool, at the K the attention curve forces you to use.
  • MuSiQue is a stress test. Results here do not automatically generalize to single-hop domains or to corpora where the evidence is not graph-structured. It is the right benchmark for making the failure visible; it is not the only benchmark that matters.
  • GraphRAG and community-based methods are related. (Edge et al., 2024) Hierarchical community summaries are a different way to assemble the envelope. Which method wins depends on corpus structure; both address the failure the table above documents.

Reproduction

python -m experiments.musique.sparql.design.vanilla_dense_positions --scope full

The script writes cache/p3v5/vanilla_dense_21k.json and the coverage table is a numpy.count_nonzero on the resulting rank arrays.
